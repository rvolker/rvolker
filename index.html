Overview

Paddles up! Chey Martineau pulled a permit for a private river trip on the "Wild" Main Salmon! We'll be launching Wednesday June 22, 2022 from Corn Creek, Idaho. At a glance, here are some facts:

  • The Main Salmon is the lower section of the Salmon River, below the infamous Middle Fork on the Salmon River which is historically known as The River of No Return. This wild river makes its way through the second-deepest gorge on the continent; only Hells Canyon on the Snake River is deeper. The Salmon's granite-walled canyon is over 1,000 feet deeper than the Grand Canyon. For approximately 180 miles, the Salmon River canyon is more than a mile deep. Another fun fact, the river gradiant is steeper than the Colorado river in the Grand Canyon.
  • What about permits? Chey Martineau pulled a permit her very first year in the river lottery.
  • Rapid rating III-IV. We can expect early season snow melt run off.
  • Will there be Tiki Torches? Why yes, or a maybe, we need to work on Chuck.
  • What do I need to bring? More details below.

What to Expect

4 rafts, 1 cataraft, 1 paddle boat, and 1 duckie to carry all of us downstream 80 miles to Carrey Creek. There will be enough rapids to making this a very exciting trip. The gradient of this river is steep, so it's all downhill towards the Pacific -it's gonna be a good flow! Even at normal flows this section of the Salmon is moving quickly, which always surprises every boater. There's no flat water, no rowing-hell like Desolation Canyon trip or the San Juan.

Private Trip. This is a self-guided private trip, which means that we get to do everything. Yep that's right, everything, and we mean everything. Sure we do everything, and that's what makes a private trip so great. There will be a lots to do every day after the thrills and spills on the river. With the fast river flow there'll be plenty of time off river to relax or go for hikes. Then the very next day we'll break down camp, load and rig the boats. Rigging the boats can take a bit of time, but once we've pushed off life is so good. There might be some pucker-factor moments so scouting the major rapids is a must, which takes time. And with rivers we know that there's only one way to go no matter what -down stream!

Shared meals. This is an opportunity to show off your best camp cooking skills. Please keep in mind that this group on previous trips has set the bar high. So do your best to impress us. Give us your best outdoor river cooking.

River. The water flow is not controlled so water level may fluctuate according to the dictates of nature. We've been watching the predictions, snow pack is below normal and the expected flow is normal or below, which means a green-light, so the trip is a go. The water temperature may be still cold to cool, this is a June trip, so please plan accordingly.

Bugs. Generally not a problem on this section of river due to flow water. However, be prepared for a mosquito hatch just in case. Pro-tip: bring bug repellent, just in case.

Climate. Daytime temperatures highs could be in the 70's or 80's with lots of sun and lows in the 40's or 50's. However, typically many early June trips experience highs in the 50's with rain and lows in the 30's. Yes, it could even snow, but we don't expect freezing temperatures. Based on historical averages, the daytime high temps could be 70-80° (78° mean) with nighttime lows somewhere in the 40-50° (46° mean). When on the river there could be full exposure to the sun, so it could be hot or cold. All things considered, the experts tell us that this is a great time of year to be on the river!

What to Know, Before You Go

  • Boating in the Main Fork of the Salmon Info from National Forest Service

Some highlights include:

  • Be Prepared. This is a river trip into the rugged wilderness. Back-country travelers must be prepared for varying, unpredictable weather conditions and should be aware of potential dangers in this environment.
  • All garbage, both organic and inorganic, will be carried out.
  • Groovers (toilets) are provided by our group at each campsite. Back-country regulations require that human waste and toilet paper be packed out of the back-country. In short, we'll all share in the duties of taking care of this nasty little necessity.
  • Respect for cultural sites. All natural objects and cultural artifacts are protected and must be left where they're found.

Pro Tips

  • Since weather conditions can be unpredictable, a small day-use dry bag to carry rain gear or sun gear that you may need on the river during the day.
  • Pack light! Dirty on the river is quite fashionable, and besides that you don't have to haul extra stuff around.
  • Small soft duffel bag to transport all your shiz to the put-in. From there you'll stuff the contents from your duffel into your river dry bag.
  • Sun glasses, and of course croakies, chums, something to keep them from floating away.
  • Pillow, small camp pillow stuffed at the bottom of your dry bag.
  • Plastic bags. (There will some available at Friday night's camp). Garbage sized for wet clothes in dry bag, and several ziplock freezer size.
  • Clothing for the car ride. Left in the car at the put-in, so after getting off the river you'll have something a little fresher to wear for the long ride home.
